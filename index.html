Gramática — Grammar Notes

Week 7 · Three key structures: stem-changing verbs, irregular YO, and porque/para/por

G1

Verbos con cambio e → ie · Stem-Changing Verbs

stem-vowel e becomes ie in all forms except nosotros — the "boot" pattern

In these verbs, the stem vowel e changes to ie in all forms except nosotros. The endings are completely regular — only the stem changes.

Pronoun querer (to want) entender (to understand) preferir (to prefer)
yo quiero entiendo prefiero
tú quieres entiendes prefieres
él/ella/Ud. quiere entiende prefiere
nosotros ✓ queremos entendemos preferimos
ellos/Uds. quieren entienden prefieren

The "boot" visual: if you draw a boot shape around the changed forms, nosotros is the only one that sits outside the boot — and it's the only one that doesn't change.

¡OJO! · Querer + infinitive

QUERER + infinitive = desire / future plan. This construction is essential for expressing goals — you will use it every week.

Quiero estudiar en México. → I want to study in Mexico.

Queremos aprender chino. → We want to learn Chinese.

Other common e → ie verbs

empezar (to begin) · pensar (to think) · cerrar (to close) · venir (to come) · sentir (to feel)

All follow the boot pattern. Remember: nosotros never changes — empezamos, pensamos, cerramos, venimos, sentimos.

G2

Formas irregulares de YO · Only the yo form is irregular!

eight common verbs · the yo form breaks the pattern · every other form is regular

For these verbs, only the yo form is irregular. Every other form (tú, él, nosotros, ellos) is conjugated normally.

Infinitive YO form Example
hacer (to do/make) hago Yo hago ejercicio cada mañana.
saber (to know facts) sé Yo sé la respuesta correcta.
conocer (to know people/places) conozco Yo conozco Madrid bien.
poner (to put/place) pongo Pongo los libros en la mochila.
salir (to go out/leave) salgo Salgo con amigos los viernes.
traer (to bring) traigo Traigo mi laptop a clase siempre.
decir (to say) digo Digo la verdad.
venir (to come) vengo Vengo de una familia bilingüe.

Saber vs. Conocer — quick recap (from Week 6)

Saber = facts, information, how to do something → Sé hablar francés. / Sé dónde está.

Conocer = people, places, familiarity → Conozco a tu hermano. / Conozco Buenos Aires.

⚠ Pitfall · Venir is "double trouble"

Venir is BOTH an e → ie stem-changer AND has an irregular yo form.

vengo · vienes · viene · venimos · vienen

G3

Porque · Para · Por · Three Confusing Words

because — in order to — because of / for / in exchange

English uses "for" and "because" loosely. Spanish keeps them apart — and choosing the right one is the difference between a clear sentence and a confusing one.

porque
because — gives a reason · + conjugated verb
Estudio porque me gusta.
No fui porque estaba enfermo.
para
in order to — purpose · + infinitive
Estudio para aprender.
Necesito un diccionario para la clase.
por
because of / for / in exchange · + noun
Lo hago por ti.
Gracias por todo.

Quick tests · Pruebas rápidas

¿Por qué? = Why? → answered with Porque = Because (one word, no space).

Para + infinitive = in order to. If you can say "in order to" in English, use para.

Por + noun = because of / for / in exchange for. Used for thanks (gracias por), travel through, time periods, and exchanges.

Cultura — Paraguay y el guaraní

Latin America's officially bilingual country · the first academy of an indigenous language in the Americas

🌿 Paraguay · A Nation That Speaks Two Languages

Paraguay is the only officially bilingual country in Latin America where the indigenous language is spoken by the majority of the population. Over 85% of Paraguayans speak guaraní — far more than the global average for indigenous languages — and many use it daily alongside Spanish. In 2011, Paraguay established the Academia de la Lengua Guaraní, the first such institution for an indigenous language in the Americas.

🗣️

El idioma · The language

Guaraní is part of the Tupí-Guaraní family and was spoken across much of South America long before Spanish arrived. Today it shares official status with Spanish in Paraguay's constitution — children grow up bilingual from birth.

📜

Una academia oficial

The Academia de la Lengua Guaraní (2011) was a landmark — for the first time in the Americas, an indigenous language received the same kind of formal academy that Spanish, French, and Italian have. It regulates spelling, vocabulary, and use in schools and government.

🌐

Palabras prestadas · Borrowed words

Spanish has borrowed many words from guaraní, including jaguar, mandioca (yuca / cassava), piraña, tapioca, and tucán. You probably already speak a little guaraní without realizing it.

💬

Jopará · Spanish + guaraní mix

Everyday Paraguayan speech often blends both languages — a phenomenon called jopará. A speaker may switch between Spanish and guaraní mid-sentence, depending on context, audience, and topic.
